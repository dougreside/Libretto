ACT 2
Scene 1.--A subterranean vault beneath the castle of Wolfenstein. (in 2.) Set floor R.H.N.E. moveable panel in upper part of R.H. flat ; sliding stone panel in lower part of L.H. flat. Wulfgar discovered chaining Rodolphe to wall of R.H. flat. Music at rise of curtain. Lights half down.
Wul.
There, my fine fellow. I think you're both safe and comfortable. No intrusion, neither light nor sound, ever come here. This should have been your lodging last night, but you see I mislaid the key.
Rod.
Wretch.
Wul.
When you want exercise or change of air, and you can manage to get out of yonder little encumbrances
[pointing to chains]
, you can take it in the vault beneath your feet. Yonder trap leads to it. You may find it a little mouldy, and may stumble over the skeleton of the last lodger. But that's nothing ; any change is better than no change at all.
Rod.
Monster, begone.
Wulfgar
(Taking up lamp)
Oh certainly--anything to oblige. In the meantime, if your exercise should give you an appetite, and you should want food and water, cry out for it--cry loud and it wont come. Ha, ha ha! Good night.
[Exits R.H. door. Lights down.
Rodolphe
Alone! So ends in darkness and in death all my bright dreams of the future. And must I perish thus--I, who have but entered the portal of life? No, no, it cannot be! I must, I will burst these bonds.
[Music. He makes an effort to break the chains.]
'Tis vain, they defy even the strength of despair.
[Shuddering.]
How awful is the chill of this noxious vault ; its icy vapors press upon my brow like the clammy hand of death, and freeze my very marrow.
[Music. Hertzog enters with dark lantern through sliding panel in L.H. flat.
Rod.
[Starting.]
Who's there?
Hert.
Thy friend.
Rod.
Who are you?
Hert.
[Turning the light of the lantern upon his own face.]
Behold!
[Chord
Rod.
[Starting.]
Hertzog, the Crook!
Hert.
Aye, so men call me.
[Puts down lantern off L.H.
Rod.
Ill-omened bird, what brings you here? Yet why should I ask? You come the minister of Death. 'Tis well, 'tis merciful-- begin your work.
Hert.
Out, foolish boy. I serve no human master.
Rod.
Then why are you here?
Hert.
I come to give thee liberty.
Rod.
Liberty?
Hert.
Aye. Listen. I hate thine enemy. Thou lov'st a maid of whom Thou lov'st a maid of whom thou hast been robbed. Would'st win her back? I have the power to aid thee.
Rodolphe
Begone, tempter. I know your power, and can guess from whence it comes. Men say you deal in dark and necromantic spells that warp the senses and enthral the soul.
Hert.
Bah! Art thou, too, tainted with the vulgar fear that calls philosophy--the natural working of great nature's laws--a spell of darkness? It is the light, weak boy, the light, which we sage men, who waste our lives oe'r midnight lamps glean from dull vapors for sluggards' use. Fools sneer the most when least they understand, and brand as foul what nature stamps as fair. Thy gains gall thee ; let me loose them.
[He touches them with his staff; they fall to the ground.
Rodolphe
[Surprised.]
What is your purpose?
Hert.
Again I say, to serve thee.
Rod.
How?
Hert.
Listen, I will tell thee a secret. She whom thou lovest is of noble birth.
Rod.
Amina?
Hert.
Aye, yet not Amina, but the only child and heiress of the noble house of Wellenstein--stolen when but an infant by a revengeful Gipsy whom her father scourged, and given to gabbling Barbara.
Rod.
Amina noble?
Hert.
Aye, and thou of humble birth. But gold can buy nobility ; nay more, can give thee power to cross thine enemy.
Rod.
Why talk to me of gold?--to me, the poorest of the poor, whose purse contains not half so much as one poor silver mark.
Hert.
Thou'rt poor indeed, but thou art poor because thou wilt be poor. 'Tis with thyself to shame the wealth of mighty Croesus.
Rod.
How?
Hert.
Hast thou not heard of glittering gold in massive piles fast locked within these mountains?
Rod.
An idle tale ; a senseless fable told by croning gossips ; A treasure often sought, but never found ; and some do say 'tis death to seek it.
Hert.
I tell thee, boy, the story's true, this gold is palpable to sight and touch, and may be garnered, too, if thou art bold enough.
Rod.
I'll hear no more! Thou'dst take advantage of my desperate strait to work some juggle to entrap my soul. Begone, I say, begone!
Hert.
And leave thee to thy doom?
Rod.
Aye.
Hert.
And thy affianced bride--would'st thou so like a craven yield her up unto thine enemy?
Rod.
Oh, agony!
Hert.
'Tis true she loves thee ; but bethink thee, boy, she is but woman, and she may be won. Her noble birth is known to Wolfenstein, who means to wed her, and with her fortune prop his falling house. Already, flattered by his serpent tounge, she dries her tears and listens to his suit.
Rod.
'Tis false!
Hert.
'Tis true!
Rod.
The proof?
Hert.
Behold!
[Music. He waves his staff; a panel slides in upper part of R.H. flat slides open, showing through gauze Wolfenstein and Amina, the former clasping the hand and kneeling at the feet of the latter whose face is half averted. Picture strongly lighted)
Rod,
[Starting toward it.]
By Heaven, he shall not!
Hert.
[Interposing; waves his staff; panel closes.]
Bravely said, bravely said.
Rod.
(Turning quickly)
What dev'lish compact would you have me sign? Propose it while my reason whirls and desperation aids your dam'd design. If by the act I snatch her from his grasp, I'll pay the ransom though it reach my soul.
[Hertzog, with face averted, chuckles sardonically)
Come, philosopher or fiend, whate'er thou art; the price, I say, the price.
Hert.
Pah! I ask no compact. I would serve thee gratis. I but demand that thou shalt serve thyself. Be rich and thou'lt be powerful. In thy revenge upon thine enemy and mine, thou'lt pay thy debt to me with interest.
Rod.
Where lies this wondrous treasure?
Hert.
Listen. Amid the fastnesses of the Hartz, beyond the outlet of the Black Gorge, lies a small lake whose waters few have ever gazen upon, for vulgar fear and superstitious dread have long since marked it for enchanted ground.
Rod.
I've heard the story.
Hert.
Trace carefully its northern shore until a rock rising like a wall bars further passage. Beneath a fringe of tangled vines you'll find a boat concealed. Behold this talismanic ring!
[Takes a large ring from his finger.]
'Tis a magnet of wondrous power. When thou hast found the boat step boldly in, this ring will guide thee safely to the entrance of the goldern cavern, within the compass of whose glittering walls thy wondering eyes may feast on wealth far greater than the coffers of the world can boast.
Rod,
Give me the ring.
Hert.
'Tis thine.
[gives it.]
Stay, thou'lt need a henchman,
[stamps his foot.]
Varlet, come forth.
[Panel in L.H. flat slides as before and Greppo enters. [aside.]
He too shall perish.
Grep.
Your will, Master?
Hert.
To part with three.
Grep.
[Aside.]
Oh, if this should be true.
[aloud]
With me!
Hert.
With thee, knave.
Grep.
Wherefore?
Hert.
I weary with thine appetite.
Greppo
[Aside.]
Here's a chance! Oh, here a chance, if he but stick to it. I must seem unwilling lest he repent,
[Aloud]
Dear master, I will reform, believe me, I will reform, allowance me to what would starve a mouse, nay, more, to pleasure you I will not eat at all. I'll live on air, but do not cast me off.
Hert.
I am resolved. Behold thy future master.
[to Rodolphe.]
Thou'lt find him faithful but he breeds a famine. Take for thy present need this purse of gold.
[offers purse; Rodolphe by a gesture refuses it.]
May, when thou art rich thou can'st repay it.
Rod.
[Taking purse.]
Nay, more, if what you say be true, eternal gratitude--
Hert.
Bah, you trifle time.
[Waves his staff, panel opens.]
yonder lies your path, it is a glorious one, begone!
Rod.
Farewell. Come, boy, come.
[Music. Exit at panel. Greppo following turns to entreat Hertzog, who raises his staff threateningly; Greppo exits hurriedly at panel.
Hert.
[Standing in opening and chuckling gazes at them.]
The thought of vegeance stirs within his heart, the lust of gold is rising in his soul, the path that leads to where 'tis hoarded ends in death. He's mine! Ha, ha, he's mine!]
