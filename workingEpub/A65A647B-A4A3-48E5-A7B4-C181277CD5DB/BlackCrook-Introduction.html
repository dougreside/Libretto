Introduction to The Black Crook

Since 1950, very few musical theater historians have seriously argued that the The Black Crook was this the first American musical. Also since 1950, many seem to believe everyone else wrongly believes it was. When discussing the piece in his seminal 1950 history, “Musical Comedy in America” Cecil Smith wrote:

The popular musical stage in the United States reached major dimensions for the first time on the evening of September 12, 1866, when the curtain of Niblo's Garden, on the corner of Boradway and Prince Street in New York, rose on the opening performance of The Black Crook. Not that The Black Crook was, as has frequently been claimed, the “first musical comedy.1

Nearly a decade later David Evans wrote in the introduction to his “The Complete Book of the American Musical”:

Since The Black Crook (1866) was the first American musical production in which there could be detected some of the ritual that the American musical theater would subsequently pursue, it is often singled out as our first musical comedy. But musical productions of other varieties had existed in this country long before The Black Crook.2

In “The Black Crook Myth,” published during The Black Crook's centenary in 1966, Julian Mates cites Smith and Ewen as evidence that “The year 1866, with its production of The Black Crook, has been accepted as the landmark indicating the beginnings of the American musical comedy.” He then goes on to demonstrate that “nothing about The Black Crook justifies its position as the precursor of our modern lyric stage; all its forms and conventions derive from a long tradition established well before 1866.”3 Every decade or so another history of the musical comes out, and almost every historian feels compelled to discuss the show at some length while at the same time observing that, whatever their less enlightened colleagues may have thought, it was in no way the “First American Musical.”

The only historian I have found who unabashedly claim that The Black Crook was the first of its' kind was is 19th century theater manager Michael Bennett Leavitt, who, in his 1912 memoir, wrote The Black Crook “was really the birth of all the ballets, burlesques, comic operas, and musical comedy of the present day.”4 Its possible Leavitt's claim still represented the popular view forty years later when Cecil Smith wrote his history of the musical, but I suspect Smith's memory was refreshed by a 1945 article in the journal Dance Index by the first curator of the Theater Collection at NYPL, George Freedley, who did not call The Black Crook first, but did claim that “Thanks to the enormous publicity it received eighty years ago and the handsome Hoboken revival fifteen years ago, this is the one musical of which even untutored audiences have heard”5

The Hoboken production to which Freedley refers was, produced in 1929 by critic and writer Christopher Moreley with choreography by Agnes De Mille and opened just two years after Florenz Ziegfeld had produced Show Boat on Broadway. The New Jersey production may have served to remind audiences and critics that there once existed another grand musical spectacle that resembled one of Ziegfelds famous Follies with its fantastic scenery and scantily clad female dancers, but which also had something like a plot. Two articles in the New York Times, in fact, compared the women in the Hoboken production to those in a Ziegfeld chorus. By the 1950s, when the first long-form histories of the modern American musical were being written, the musical had come to be largely defined by the model created by Rodgers and Hammerstein beginning with Oklahoma (which also featured Agnes De Mille choreography) and presaged by Show Boat. Perhaps Morley's production reminded audiences that Show Boat itself grew out of a tradition that included The Black Crook, and believed Leavitt's claim that it was the earliest of its kind.

Still, the caveats of a chorus of musical theater historians are correct: The Black Crook was not the first musical (at least in the way the genre is popularly defined today). The score changed frequently—not only from production to production but even in the course of the original run. The music, dances, and songs were swapped out for others as the cast changed or when the producers felt the program needed to be refreshed. An 1892 piece in the New York Times noted that a “'variety show' was introduced in the third act of the spectacle sometime in the seventies”6, which explains why later programs promise acts by the trapeze artists such as the “Kiralfy Brothers” (1886) or, in the fourth production in the 1870s--“Mr. Felix Regamey, the Great Caricaturist.”7
Certainly, it seems author, Charles Barras, did not intend for the production to include so many diversions from his plot. The author of an 1885 piece in the New York Sun recalls that Barras “fairly cried out aloud when his labored and sophomorical dialogue was cut out by the page in reducing his play to a spectacle.”8 The play had been licensed, however, for exactly the purpose of, in the words of box office manager Joseph Whitton, “a clothes-lines on which to hang the pretty dresses.”9 In the early summer of 1866 impressarios Henry Jarrett and Harry Palmer had joined with William Wheatley, manager of the Niblo's Garden Theater, to look for a play that could accommodate the ballet dancers and scenery the pair had engaged on a tour of Europe in the winter of 1865-1866.10 Jarrett and Palmer had originally planned to use the speciality acts in a production of Undine they planned to stage at the Academy of Music, but during their trip it seems they had been so impressed by a Parisian staging of La Biche Au Bois they had decided to produce it instead. Neither was to be, however, for the Academy of Music burned down in May of 1866, just as the pair returned from Europe. They went to Wheatley with the idea of producing the public domain opera, “The Naiad Queen” at Niblo's Garden, but the manager objected on the grounds that if it were a success “every concert saloon in New York” would stage their own competing productions11. The trio agreed to look for an appropriate piece for which they could obtain an exclusive license and found one several days later when theater manager, Leonard Grover, suggested Wheatley consider a new play by Barras for his purposes.12 Wheatley was convinced, and though Jarrett and Palmer initially balked at the fee Barras demanded (feeling that for less money they could commission a new play by fellow manager and playwright Augustin Daly), they eventually agreed to Barras's terms.13
Once the rights were obtained, the producers immediately began to look for moments into which to insert dance and spectacle. The text provided many such opportunities. The Harvard Manuscript copy includes four passages laid out as verse: The choral procession that closes the first scene (“Hark! Hark! Hark!”), the Incantation, the chorus of Fairies, and Stalacta's looking-glass spell). Further, in the fourth scene of the first act, there is an obvious song cue when the chamber maid, Carline, concludes a monologue saying “I'm so happy I could sing for a month” and Stalacta is directed to sing a song to announce her entrance. More importantly given the talent Jarret and Palmer had hired in Europe, there are also many moments in the text that call for dance. In the first scene, the villagers decide to practice their “festival dance” (described as a “Garland Dance” in the stage directions). The Fairy Queen Stalacta commands a dance by the water fairies in honor of her birthday and later in the scene a “grotesque dance” is commanded for the entertainment of Rodolphe and Greppo. The stage directions at the opening of Act Four (Count Wolfenstein's birthday ball) calls for a dance at a masked ball. Finally, the play ends with a “transformation scene” that features a march by the Amazons.
These moments can be mapped without much difficulty onto the acts described in the opening night program:

  • Pas de Sabot/Pas de Fleurs ....Garland Dance/Exit of chorus
  • Song: Naughty, Naughty Man....Carline's song
  • Grand Incantation....Hertzog's Summoning Incantation
  • Pas de Naide....Stalacta's birthday dance
  • Pas de Demon....Grotesque dance
  • Bal Masque/Pas Espanoil/Pas Hongroise....Dance at the ball
  • Dance de Amazons....Rescue of Rudolphe by Stalacta's army in scene 4
  • Transformation Scene....Transformation in scene 7 and 8

These moments, although imagined by Barras, seem to have been greatly expanded in the original production (references to dance throughout his manuscript copy are emended to refer specifically to full ballet performances).

When the first performance lasted for over five hours, it was clear cuts had to be made. Barras may have objected, but all of the reviews confirmed that the text was the most disposable element of the piece. Authorized by the initial popular appreciation of the spectacle and critical dismissal of the text, later productions interpolated acts even less relevant to Barras's plot (and deletions in the prompt books suggest entire scenes of Barras's script were sometimes dropped). When the 1871 revival of The Black Crook closed at Niblo’s Garden in February of 1872, it included, according to a March 2 article in The Spirit of the Times, a “great equestrian goat,” “trained animals”, a “clown dog”, “youthful gymnasts” and an “anaconda.”14

Nevertheless, despite frequent changes in the music, the script used as a starting point seems to have remained remarkably consistent (at least while the show was performed in the United States). Indeed, a writer for the New York Times mocked Wheatley for changing the ballet while leaving the text, which the reporter clearly thought inferior, untouched (28 May 1867). Twentieth entury Scripts now held by the Library of Congress and used in Federal Theater Project productions in the 1930s are almost identical to Charles Barras's draft manuscript preserved in the Harvard Theater Collection. The Black Crook may have been somewhat innovative, then, in bringing variety into a American theater filled with an audience prepared to listen to a play. Certainly this was the opinion of the author of an unsigned March 28, 1874 article in the New York Times, the journalist argued that The Black Crook, “being called a drama, gave an excuse to see a variety performance, against which a ridiculous prejudice had long existed.”

The Black Crook and several of the American musical theater pieces that followed (A Trip to Chinatown and L. Frank Baum's 1903 adaptation of his Wizard of Oz for example) created spaces for a wide variety of musical styles to be performed within productions with with relatively fixed scripts (somewhat legitimizing the extravaganzas as drama). Most earlier forms of American music theater did not permit such an eclectic mix of music to be connected to a single plot: the scores of comic operas and operetta are intended to be coherent musical pieces and the music therefore had to be more or less of a piece and even The Beggars Opera (which interpolated popular songs into a stable text) did not permit such a wide range of musical styles as The Black Crook. By the time the scores of the American musical as we know it today began to emerge in the second and third decades of the 20th century, the genre permitted an increasingly wide array of possible styles for songs. Show Boat could therefore include a pastiche of African-American spiritual in “Old Man River” and the European-flavored “Life Upon the Wicked Stage.” For this reason, it may not be so ridiculous to start the story of the American musical with Black Crook, after all.

Introduction to the variorum edition

This edition preserves the five extant 19th century copies of the Barras's text: a manuscript in Barras's hand (preserved in notebooks held at the Harvard Theater Collection), three 19th century manuscript prompt books held by the Harvard Theater Collection, New York Public Library, the Hampden-Booth Theater Library at the Player's Club, and a published, printed edition published in Chicago in 1867 and now held by the Library of Congress. It does not include Myron Matlaw's 1967 edition (published in his anthology The Black Crook and Other Nineteenth Century American Plays), typescripts of productions performed for the Federal Theater Project during the depression, or the two typescripts, seeming created by the staff of New York Public Library and later micro-formed as part of the Nineteenth Century American Drama series. The NYPL typescripts transcribe a copyright date of 1866 and a Buffalo publisher, and so may preserve the text of a no longer extant exition, but it seems just as likely that these were simply edited transcripts of the promptbook still in the collection (which, like the other 19th century promptbooks, has the title page from the 1866 Buffalo edition pasted onto its first pages).

This digital edition also excludes any copies of the London libretto which has nothing but the title in common with Charles Barras's play.

Of the versions included in this variorum edition, The Harvard Theater Collection manuscript is likely the earliest. Barras dated the text “1862” on the title page, but the “2” appears to have been written over an earlier number, leading Craig Bowley15 to propose in his 1975 master's thesis that Barras may have backdated the piece to avoid accusations of plagiarizing other similar works. Instructions to “return to Mr. Barras” are written on the front the start of each folio, and manuscript emendations to the text suggest edits in the manuscript were made during reheasrals (e.g. references to a bridge in the first scene are struck, likely as result of a set design that differed slightly from the one Barras initially imagined). The second earliest copy is probably also held by Harvard, and seems to represent a prompt book for the original production (it includes, among the end papers, a receipt for $100 paid by Charles Barras to John Holloway and dated October 2, 1866 for “composing + arranging orchestra parts for “The Black Crook”). The prompt copy held by NYPL is also likely an early one produced by the same copyist who produced the Harvard prompt copy (the handwriting and paper are very similar in both). The prompt copy at the Hampden-Booth Theater library may also have been produced by the same copyist, but appears to have been used in an 1867 tour of the Midwest (there are scene timings for an July 24, 1867 stop at the Metropolitan Theater in Indianapolis and a signature by a prompter, P.C. Nagen who used the book in Chicago in 1869. As noted above, all three promptbooks have pasted onto their first page a printed copy of the copyright notice and title page from the no-longer-extant 1866 Buffalo printing (which may have predated the production at Niblos). This title page resembles the typeface and format of the Chicago edition so closely as to suggest that the Chicago edition was essentially a reprint of the Buffalo version with the cuts made for the various productions excised.16

This digital edition also features new recordings and sheet music for eight pieces of music published in the late 1860s which were marketed as featuring music from the 1866 production of The Black Crook. To hear any of this music simply click the speaker icon within the text. Likewise, tapping the musical note icons will allow the reader to study the sheet music. Note though, that, with the exception of You Naughty, Naughty Men, none of these pieces likely represent anything like the piano-vocal score used in the production. They are, rather, short arrangements to be played at home featuring some of the melodies audience may have heard during the original production. The Black Crook Waltz, for instance, includes four sections: “The Power of Love” (an excerpt from an aria from Balfe's opera Satanella) and pieces labeled “Bonfanti”, “Sangalli”, and “Betty Regal” (three of the principal dancers in the original production). Each of these are likely just a brief excerpts of the full dance melody which is now lost. Any attempt to place the rest of the published pieces into the action of the play is mostly guess work, but I have tried to find moments where the music fits the spirit of the moment. My rationale for the placement, admittedly very hypothetical in some cases, for each placement is below.

Finally, please note that this variorum edition should be considered a draft, prepared as a test case for the prototype app you are using to read this text. Please report any errors in the transcription or encoding to Doug Reside (dougreside@nypl.org).

Program Description: Pas de Fleurs
Published Sheet Music: Black Crook Waltzes
Rationale: The sheet music, arranged by Joseph Noll, claims to be a selection of ballets from the Black Crook. It seems possible that at least a fragment of this ballet (which the New York Times reports was encored twice) should be included in this piece.

Program Description: Pas de Sabot
Published Sheet Music: Black Crook Galop
Rationale: Although later productions used a galop for the demon dance in Stalacta's grotto, it seems possible this Thomas Baker's Galop was used earlier. David Costa is credited with the “Pas De Demon” on the program, and although he is usually credited on sheet music for pieces with which he was connected, he is not mentioned on the published music for The Black Crook Galop. Further he is not mentioned on the program in connection with the Pas De Sabot (or a “Dance of the Wooden Shoes”). The pounding rhythm of this Galop suggests it might fit a “Pas De Sabot.” Further, it is almost possible to set the lyrics of the Villagers chorus (“Hark! Hark! Hark!”) to this music if the lyric “Hark the birds with tuneful voices” begins on the fifth measure. In the program the “Pas de Sabot” is listed before the “Pas De Fleurs” (which would put it too early for the Villager's chorus). However, it seems possible that it accompanied the Villagers' entrance and then was reprised with lyrics on their exit. It is also suggestive, though by no means conclusive proof of a change from the order in the program, that the New York Times critic mentions the Pas de Sabot after the Pas de Fleur in his synopsis of the play.11

Program Description: Naughty, Naughty Man
Published Sheet Music: You Naughty, Naughty Men
Rationale: The similarity in the title and the fact that published sheet music suggests that the song was sung by Millie Cavendish (who played Carline) makes the placement of this one a fairly sure bet.

Program Description: Pas de Naide including the songs “Flow on Silver Stream” and “The Power of Love”/Pas de Demon
Published Sheet Music: Black Crook Waltz
Rationale: Within the Black Crook Waltz there are passages labeled “The Power of Love” and with the names of the dancers (Bonfanti, Sangalli, and Betty Regal [sic]) who are credited as prinicipals in the Pas de Naide and the Pas de Demon. Note that the music for The Power of Love is from Balfe's opera Satanella.

Program Description: Bal Masque / Pas Espanoil
Published Sheet Music: Black Crook Lancers
Rationale: The directions in the printed sheet music require male dancers to dance with female ones. This is the only place in the place in the script where this seems possible.

Program Description: Pas Hongroise
Published Sheet Music: Mazeri Mazurka
Rationale: Text on the cover of the Mazzeri Mazurka credits the Mazzeri and Zuccolli, and Mademoiselle Zuardi with dancing this number. The Pas Hongroise is the only dance on the program where these three names are mentioned.

Program Description: Dance de Amazons
Published Sheet Music: March of the Amazons
Rationale: This Amazon dance is credited to David Costa in both the program and the sheet music.

Program Description: Transformation Scene
Published Sheet Music: Fairy Queen March / Transformation Polka
Rationale: The name of the Transformation Polka suggests that it was used in the transformation scene in the fourth act. Prior to the actual transformation, though, the stage directions mention that the Amazons perform a “characteristic march.” Perhaps this was the Fairy Queen March.
